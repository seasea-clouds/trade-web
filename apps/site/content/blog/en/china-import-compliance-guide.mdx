## Every Product Has a Compliance Path

China's import regulations are not one-size-fits-all. A shipment of French wine faces different requirements than German electronics or Korean cosmetics. Understanding which regulations apply to your specific product is the first and most important step in any China market entry strategy.

This guide helps you identify the compliance requirements that match your product category.

## Food and Beverage Products

### What Applies

If you export food, beverages, agricultural products, or dietary supplements to China, two primary requirements apply:

**GACC Registration (Decree 248)** — Every overseas food manufacturing, processing, and storage facility must register with the General Administration of Customs of China. This is mandatory for all food products entering China, from raw ingredients to finished packaged goods.

**Chinese Label Compliance (GB 7718)** — All imported food products must carry a Chinese-language label that meets GB 7718 standards. The label must include product name, ingredients list, net content, distributor information, country of origin, date of manufacture, and shelf life.

### Categories at a Glance

| Product Type | Primary Requirement | Secondary Requirement |
|-------------|-------------------|---------------------|
| Packaged food | GACC registration | Chinese label (GB 7718) |
| Beverages (wine, coffee, tea) | GACC registration | Chinese label (GB 7718) |
| Dairy products | GACC registration (18 categories) | Chinese label + health cert |
| Health supplements | GACC registration + review | Chinese label (special rules) |
| Raw ingredients | GACC registration | Chinese label (GB 7718) |
| Pet food | GACC registration | Label compliance + MOA registration |

## Electronics, Appliances, and Industrial Products

### What Applies

**CCC Certification** — China Compulsory Certification (CCC) is mandatory for 17 product categories including electrical appliances, IT equipment, automotive parts, lighting, and toys. Products within these categories cannot be imported, sold, or used in China without a valid CCC certificate.

Products outside the mandatory CCC list may still require voluntary certification or type-testing, depending on the specific industry standards.

### Categories at a Glance

| Product Type | Primary Requirement | Notes |
|-------------|-------------------|-------|
| Home appliances | CCC certification | 17 mandatory categories |
| IT equipment | CCC certification | Most categories require CCC |
| Automotive parts | CCC certification | Safety-related parts |
| Lighting products | CCC certification | Mandatory categories |
| Toys and children's products | CCC certification | Specific toy categories |
| Industrial machinery | Voluntary certification | Type-testing may apply |
| Components and parts | Depends on end use | Check with a specialist |

## Cosmetics and Personal Care

### What Applies

**NMPA Cosmetics Filing or Registration** — The National Medical Products Administration (NMPA) regulates all cosmetics imported into China. The requirements depend on whether your product is classified as "ordinary cosmetics" or "special cosmetics."

**Ordinary cosmetics** — Products like shampoos, skin cleansers, makeup, and fragrances require an NMPA filing (notification) before they can be imported. The process involves product testing, safety assessment, and submission through the NMPA online system.

**Special cosmetics** — Products with claims related to sun protection, hair dyeing, freckle removal, and anti-hair loss require full NMPA registration, which is a more intensive process involving clinical trials and a longer review period.

### Categories at a Glance

| Product Type | Classification | Primary Requirement |
|-------------|---------------|-------------------|
| Skincare (moisturizers, serums) | Ordinary | NMPA filing |
| Makeup (foundation, lipstick) | Ordinary | NMPA filing |
| Sunscreen products | Special | NMPA registration |
| Hair dye products | Special | NMPA registration |
| Body wash, shampoo | Ordinary | NMPA filing |
| Fragrances and perfumes | Ordinary | NMPA filing |
| Anti-hair loss products | Special | NMPA registration |

## Cross-Border E-Commerce Products

### What Applies

**Cross-Border E-Commerce (CBEC) Compliance** — Products sold through cross-border e-commerce channels (Tmall Global, JD Worldwide, Douyin Global, Kaola) follow a different regulatory path than traditional imports.

CBEC allows foreign brands to sell directly to Chinese consumers without requiring a Chinese entity for import clearance. However, products must still comply with:

- **Positive list compliance** — Only products on the CBEC positive list can be sold through this channel
- **Personal use declaration** — Each transaction is treated as personal use, not commercial import
- **Label requirements** — Electronic labels are accepted in some cases
- **Brand authorization** — Proof of brand ownership or authorization is required

### Categories at a Glance

| Product Type | CBEC Eligible | Notes |
|-------------|--------------|-------|
| Cosmetics | Yes | NMPA filing still required |
| Food supplements | Yes | GACC registration still required |
| Baby formula | Yes | Strict registration requirements |
| Electronics | Yes | CCC certification still required |
| Fashion and accessories | Yes | Generally lower compliance burden |
| Household goods | Yes | Varies by product |

## Brand Protection: The Universal Requirement

### What Applies

**China Trademark Registration** — China operates a first-to-file trademark system. This means the first person to register a trademark in China owns the rights, regardless of who used it first globally. If your brand is not registered in China, you risk losing the right to use your own brand name.

Trademark registration is not optional for any company serious about the Chinese market. It protects your brand against counterfeiting, enables you to sell through major e-commerce platforms, and gives you legal standing to enforce your rights.

This requirement applies to **every product category** — food, electronics, cosmetics, fashion, industrial goods, and everything in between.

## How to Determine Your Compliance Path

Follow these steps to identify the regulations that apply to your product:

**Step 1: Identify your product category** — Determine whether your product is food, electronics, cosmetics, general consumer goods, or industrial equipment. This determines the primary regulatory framework.

**Step 2: Check mandatory requirements** — Confirm whether your product falls under any mandatory compliance programs (GACC for food, CCC for electronics, NMPA for cosmetics).

**Step 3: Assess label and packaging requirements** — Almost all imported products require some form of Chinese labeling. The specific requirements vary by product category.

**Step 4: Evaluate your sales channel** — Traditional import and cross-border e-commerce follow different rules. Your distribution strategy affects which requirements apply.

**Step 5: Register your trademark** — Regardless of product category, trademark registration should be completed before or in parallel with your compliance process.

## Common Misconceptions

### "My products are already sold in other countries, so China compliance should be similar."

China's regulatory system is distinct. Products approved in the EU, US, or Japan do not automatically qualify for China. Each product must go through the specific China compliance process.

### "Cross-border e-commerce means I don't need compliance."

Cross-border e-commerce reduces some barriers but does not eliminate compliance requirements. Product-specific regulations like GACC registration, CCC certification, and NMPA filing still apply to products sold through CBEC channels.

### "I only need one type of compliance."

Most products require multiple compliance steps. A food product needs both GACC registration and label compliance. An electronic product may need CCC certification, label compliance, and trademark registration. Compliance is rarely a single-step process.

### "The compliance process is the same for all countries."

Different countries have different procedures for interacting with Chinese regulators. Some have mutual recognition agreements, while others require direct submission. The process varies based on your country of origin.
